Hack your DX

Print custom DX labels to use with expired or hand-rolled film.

  1. Strip the original DX pattern off the cassette — scrape or sand all 12 contact pads down to bare metal so the camera can't read the old code.
  2. Print the new DX label on sticker paper (or paper + tape).
  3. Cut out every white square from the printed grid — those become the metal contacts the camera will read.
  4. Glue the label onto the cassette aligned with the original contact strip. Black squares stay covered, cut-out squares expose the bare metal underneath.

What is DX encoding?

DX (Digital indeX) encoding is a standard introduced by Kodak in 1983 for 35 mm film cassettes. A 12-cell silver-and-black grid printed on the side of the canister tells the camera the film's ISO/DIN speed, length (number of exposures), and exposure tolerance. Electrical contacts inside the camera read the pattern automatically — this is called CAS (Camera Auto Sensing).

What is this simulator for?

  • Decode any DX code by clicking squares to match what you see on a real cassette.
  • Encode by picking ISO, length, and tolerance — the grid updates to match.
  • Hack expired or bulk-loaded film: print a custom DX sticker to trick your camera into using the right ISO when the original code is wrong or missing.
  • Learn how analog cameras "talk" to film without any electronics inside the cassette.

How to read the grid

Cells 1 and 7 are always silver (electrical ground). Cells 2–6 encode ISO speed, 8–10 encode film length, 11–12 encode exposure tolerance. Silver = contact (1), black = no contact (0).
